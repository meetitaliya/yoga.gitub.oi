Bald Eagle

The bald eagle is a majestic bird of prey that is widely recognized as a symbol of the United States.

Physical Characteristics

  • Size: Bald eagles are large birds, with wingspans ranging from 6 to 7.5 feet and weighing 6 to 14 pounds.
  • Coloration: Despite its name, bald eagles are not actually bald. They have a distinctive white head and tail, with a dark brown body.
  • Beak and Talons: They have a powerful hooked beak and sharp talons used for hunting and catching prey.

Behavior and Habitat

  • Habitat: Bald eagles are found near bodies of open water, including lakes, rivers, and coastlines, across North America.
  • Feeding Habits: They are opportunistic hunters and scavengers, primarily feeding on fish, but also consuming birds, mammals, and carrion.
  • Nesting: Bald eagles build large nests known as aeries, often in tall trees near water. They are known to use the same nest for multiple years, expanding it over time.

Diet

Bald eagles are carnivores and mainly feed on fish. They use their sharp talons to snatch fish from the water's surface.

Conservation Status

The bald eagle population has rebounded significantly since being listed as "Endangered" in the United States. It is now classified as "Least Concern" by the International Union for Conservation of Nature (IUCN).

Interesting Facts

  • National Symbol: The bald eagle is the national bird and emblem of the United States, representing freedom and strength.
  • Longevity: Bald eagles can live for up to 20-30 years in the wild, and even longer in captivity.
  • Distinctive Call: Bald eagles have a high-pitched, melodious call that is often associated with their presence.
